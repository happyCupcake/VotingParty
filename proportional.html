Amid the ongoing quest for solutions to political polarization, it becomes paramount to scrutinize the backdrop in which political decisions unfold. This entails delving into how distinct political systems interact with and potentially amplify underlying psychological dynamics.

Notably, the United States stands out for its political landscape dominated by just two major parties. This outcome is intricately linked to the "winner take all" or "first past the post" voting system. In contrast, numerous other nations employ a proportional or mixed electoral system. In these systems, a party's share of the popular vote directly translates into a corresponding portion of seats within a representative body.

For instance, a party securing 5 percent of the vote secures 5 percent of the seatsâ€”a far cry from the potential non-representation often observed in the U.S. This discrepancy could potentially exacerbate the prevalent "us-and-them" dynamic within the American political landscape.

Although systematic evidence contrasting identity-based politics with the U.S. political system remains elusive for now, this avenue of exploration is critical. Extensive cross-country research, while challenging to gather, holds the key to comprehending the potential impact of diverse voting systems on polarization.

Yet, glimmers of insight shine through. Evidence suggests that more proportional systems correlate with higher voter turnout, especially among supporters of smaller parties. Intriguingly, this surge in turnout corresponds with a heightened sense of voters believing their ballot wields influence.

While proportional voting isn't a definitive solution to polarization, it could hinder extreme viewpoints from dominating the political discourse. As we navigate the labyrinth of polarization, the concept of proportional voting emerges as a thought-provoking component. Delving into how different electoral systems intertwine with our psychological landscape adds a new layer to our quest for cohesive governance.
