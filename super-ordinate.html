Within the intricate tapestry of human interactions, a powerful concept emerges—superordinate goals. This psychological principle posits that resolving identity-based conflicts hinges on the cultivation of shared objectives and a broader, all-encompassing sense of self. By nurturing a higher-level identity, we have the potential to bridge divisions and nurture unity.

This approach finds historical resonance in how leaders throughout time have harnessed rituals to forge unity among diverse cultures. Yet, this path requires a cautious tread. The forging of a cohesive in-group can inadvertently give rise to out-groups, a phenomenon poignantly underscored by Richard Dawkins. His cautionary words highlight the perils of unchecked national pride, invoking historical figures such as Hitler and Trump.

Dawkins' observations beckon us to reflect: Can we distill our identity down to pure humanity, or does a unified "humanity" veer into abstraction? In the broader discourse, the skepticism voiced by former British Prime Minister Theresa May toward a universalist outlook introduces nuance. May cautioned against laying claim to global citizenship, revealing the delicate balance we must navigate.

Research further unveils intriguing insights. A recent comprehensive review spearheaded by Sam McFarland delves into the implications of embracing a universalist perspective. Their findings illuminate a fascinating link: a robust identification with global citizenship often correlates with heightened empathy.

Yet, the intricate interplay between heightened empathy and identification remains a puzzle, a dynamic yet to be fully untangled. As we navigate the realm of superordinate goals and collective identity, a path to enlightenment begins to emerge. The journey toward unity may lie in embracing a grander sense of self—one that transcends borders and champions humanity.

Our exploration of this intricate interplay between identity, unity, and empathy continues, offering prospects for a more harmonious world.
